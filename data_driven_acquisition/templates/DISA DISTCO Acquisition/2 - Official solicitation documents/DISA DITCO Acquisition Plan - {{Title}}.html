ACQUISITION STRATEGY (AS)/ACQUISITION PLAN (AP)

(Final [Insert Date])

 

The Contract Planner shall prepare written Acquisition Plans for:

- Acquisitions for cost-reimbursement type contracts, as defined in FAR 16.3;

- Acquisitions for development, as defined in FAR 35.001, when the total cost of all contracts for the acquisition program is estimated at $10 million or more;

- Acquisitions for production or services when the total cost of all contracts for the acquisition program is estimated at $50 million or more for all years or $25 million or more for any fiscal year; and

- Any other acquisition considered appropriate by the department or agency.

- Prepare written acquisition plans for acquisition programs meeting the above thresholds on a program basis. Other acquisition plans may be written on either a program or an individual contract basis.

- The program manager, or other official responsible for the program, has overall responsibility for acquisition planning.

 

Written plans are not required in acquisitions for a final buy out or a one-time buy. The terms "final buy out" and "one-time buy" refer to a single contract that covers all known present and future requirements. This exception does not apply to a multi-year contract or a contract with options or phases.

For information, the definitions of Will Cost and Should Cost are provided here:

 

Will Cost: Latest Cost Estimate selected by the MDA for the program-typically the Independent Cost Estimate. The Will Cost estimate is the official position of the Department for use in budgeting, programming, setting acquisition baselines, and for any other program reporting requirements external to the Department.

 

Should Cost: Internal target estimate (lower than the Will Cost) established/updated by Program Manager for each Milestone decision, or when otherwise directed by the MDA. Should Cost are compared against the Will Cost Estimate by fiscal year. Should cost estimates are developed based upon one, or more, of the following methods: use of detailed bottom-up estimates; identification of discrete measurable items/efforts that can achieve savings against the Will Cost estimate; and projected savings from competitive/incentive contracting strategies and contract negotiations.

A.5 Delivery or Performance-Period Requirements. Describe the basis for establishing delivery or performance period requirements. Explain and provide reasons for any urgency if it results in concurrency of development and production or constitutes justification for not providing for full and open competition.

 

Planners and COs should be realistic and practicable in establishing delivery and/or performance schedules. Schedules which are too limited could result in counterproductive outcomes that restrict competition, are detrimental to small business policies, or result in higher contract costs. Delivery or Performance incentives can be used to stimulate early performance and/or delivery in addition to the agreed upon schedule or term expressly stated in the contract.

This should include the consequences of trade-offs associated with capability or performance characteristics and various cost, performance and schedule goals with the objective of achieving the best balance among these elements.

 

A.7 Risks. Discuss technical, cost, schedule, and supply chain management risks and describe what efforts are planned or underway to reduce risk and the consequences of failure to achieve goals. If concurrency of development and production is planned, discuss its effects on cost and schedule risks. Schedule risk is risk of contractor delivering products and services per the contract delivery schedule; it is not related to risk of awarding contract by a certain date.

 

The reliance on commercial products and services, open systems architecture, and speed of deployment has contributed to the need to actively manage risk in acquisition programs. If concurrency of production and developmental efforts are planned, the Planner should discuss the application of evolutionary or spiral development methodologies and the particular risk mitigation techniques employed. (Note: If the requirement is for Information Technology (IT) or National Security System (NSS), discuss risk mitigation under B.19, and provide a reference note here to that location)

 

Supply Chain Risk Management (SCRM) must be considered whenever acquiring Information, Communications and Technology (ICT) systems, services, supplies and/or supporting infrastructure. SCRM is the systematic process for managing supply chain risk by identifying susceptibilities, vulnerabilities and threats throughout a supply chain in an effort to develop associated mitigation strategies - whether presented by the Contractor, the supplied product and its subcomponents, or the supply chain across the entire system lifecycle (e.g., Design and Engineering, Manufacturing and Assembly, Distribution and Warehousing, Operations and Support, and Disposal and Return). Specific guidance for addressing SCRM is under development by the DISA CIAE. Please contact the Acquisition Support Center (Group Phone: 301-225-4150 or disa.meade.cae.list.acqusition-support-center-group@mail.mil) for more information on addressing SCRM in your Combined Acquisition Strategy/Acquisition Plan.

 

 

A.8 Acquisition Streamlining. If the program is designated by the requiring agency as a program subject to acquisition streamlining, discuss plans to encourage industry participation, select and tailor only the necessary and cost-effective requirements, and state the timeframe for identifying which of those specifications and standards, originally provided for guidance only, shall become mandatory.

 

a. General Guidance. The purpose of streamlining is to reduce unnecessary practices and procedures and thereby reduce the overall acquisition costs. One method that can be used is to encourage the exchange of information with industry by holding industry or small business conferences, pre-proposal and pre-solicitation conferences, site visits, etc. Any form of electronic transmission of acquisition documents which increases efficiency and/or effectiveness of acquisition processes can be construed as an acquisition streamlining approach for the project/program.

 

In addition to those requirements under the references listed, discussion under this paragraph should include consideration of the following: electronic solicitation, oral presentations / proposals, fair opportunity considerations, etc. The use of an IPT is an additional streamlining method that can be used in the acquisition process.

 

b. Commercial Practices. Agencies are encouraged to procure commercial items to capitalize on technical advances in the marketplace. In general this practice has reduced development costs while yielding a product or service that meets the customer's needs. Discuss the benefits from the use of commercial practices which may include:

●       Simplified acquisition procedures will be used to the maximum extent possible.

●       Contracting activity may combine the synopsis and the solicitation as one document.

●       Solicitations for commercial items do not have to include sub-factors or describe the relative weight of the factors.

●       COs should allow offerors to propose more than one product to meet the Government's needs.

●       Reliance on the contractor's quality assurance systems.

 

If applicable, consider stating "No commercial items are anticipated in the instant acquisition."

SECTION B - PLAN OF ACTION

 

B.1 Sources. Indicate the prospective sources of supplies or services (commercial vendors, government sources, etc.) that can meet the Government's needs. Consider required sources of supplies or services (see Part 8) (AbilityOne, NIB/NISH, etc.) and sources identifiable through databases including the Government-wide database of contracts and other procurement instruments intended for use by multiple agencies available at www.contractdirectory.gov/contractdirectory/. Include consideration of small business, veteran-owned small business, service-disabled veteran-owned small business, HUBZone small business, small disadvantaged business, and women-owned small business concerns (see Part 19), and the impact of any bundling that might affect their participation in the acquisition (see 7.107) (15 U.S.C. 644(e)). When the proposed acquisition strategy involves bundling, identify the incumbent contractors and contracts affected by the bundling. Address the extent and results of the market research and indicate the impact on the various elements of the plan (see Part 10).

 

One of the most critical phases in the acquisition planning process is the identification of sources that can meet the Agency's needs. The sources range from non-mandatory to statutory sources, e.g., small business and small disadvantaged business. Discuss the following:

 

a. Market Research: Discuss market research methodologies used and findings that impact the procurement, such as determinations to use commercial or Government resources, lease versus buy, functions that may be inherently governmental, set asides, General Services Administration (GSA) contracts, etc.

 

b. Interagency Acquisitions Under The Economy Act: Discuss consideration given to fulfilling the requirement by other than an existing DISA contract vehicle or a DISA contracting office. Orders for products or services provided under another agency's contracts require compliance with the Economy Act. The requirements office should determine Economy Act applicability in the initial stages of the acquisition planning process. Government-Wide Acquisition Contracts (GWACs) and Franchise Fund Pilot Programs (FFPP) are exempt from the Economy Act and are subject to different statutory authorities (See DARS Subpart 17.5).

 

c. Small Business: It is the policy of the Federal Government to place a fair proportion of the contracts with small business (15 U.S.C. 631(a)). The planner is therefore required to structure the requirement to encourage Small Business participation, and to give consideration to Small Disadvantaged Businesses (SDBs), HUBZones, Women-Owned Small Business (WOSB), Service Disabled Veteran Owned Small Business, and other small business concerns. Similarly, FAR 19.202-2 requires the CO to make every effort to locate small businesses. The planner must also discuss small business opportunities with regard to the Subcontracting Plan.

 

d. Bundling and Consolidation: Address the extent and results of the market research and indicate their impact on small businesses. When the AP involves bundling and/or consolidation, the business size of incumbent contractors should be addressed in A.1.b.

 

Bundling and Consolidation of contract requirements are methods used to reduce cost to the Government through economies of scale and reduced administrative cost to award and monitor the contract. However, due to their adverse impact on small businesses, market research shall be accomplished to determine if consolidation or bundling can be supported. The Planner shall include the appropriate justification or business case in the AP, setting forth measurable and substantial benefits to be realized. A Determination and Findings (D&F), approved by the HCA or SPE as appropriate (see DARS 207.170), is required for consolidation of requirements, and shall accompany the AP through the approval process.

 

B.2 Competition. Describe how competition will be sought, promoted, and sustained throughout the course of the acquisition. The competition strategy for this acquisition, as well as future acquisitions, must be addressed. If full and open competition is not contemplated, cite the authority in FAR 6.302 or FAR 8.405-6 (Limited Sources), discuss the basis for the application of that authority, identify the source(s), and discuss why full and open competition cannot be obtained. Identify the major components or subsystems. Discuss component breakout plans relative to these major components or subsystems. Describe how competition will be sought, promoted, and sustained for these components or subsystems. Describe how competition will be sought, promoted, and sustained for spares and repair parts. Identify the key logistic milestones, such as technical data delivery schedules and acquisition method coding conferences that affect competition. When effective subcontract competition is both feasible and desirable, describe how such subcontract competition will be sought, promoted, and sustained throughout the course of the acquisition. Identify any known barriers to increase competition (both prime and subcontractor) and address how to overcome them.

 

In this section of the AP, address the extent of competition obtained in past solicitations, how full and open competition will be obtained for this acquisition, or, if procuring on an other than full and open competitive basis, cite the exception authority (see FAR 6.302 or FAR 8.405-6 (Limited Sources)), and identify the source or limited number of sources being solicited. Also, for sole source or limited competitions, the AP should address plans to promote competition in the future. Refer to the DARS Part 6 for guidance on preparing and selecting the applicable template to support an other-than-full-and-open competition justification and approval (J&A). Also include if any involvement or limitations on foreign contractors are being allowed to participate at the prime contractor or subcontractor level.

 

B.3 Contract Type Selection. Discuss the rationale for the selection of contract type. For other than firm-fixed-price contracts, see 16.103(d) for additional documentation guidance. Acquisition personnel shall document the acquisition plan with findings that detail the particular facts and circumstances, (e.g., complexity of the requirements, uncertain duration of the work, contractors technical capability and financial responsibility, or adequacy of the contractors accounting system), and associated reasoning essential to support the contract type selection. The contracting officer shall ensure that requirements and technical personnel provide the necessary documentation to support the contract type selection.

 

This paragraph should contain the contract type and justification for its use, and must be coordinated with the CO who has the final responsibility for that determination. In doing so, the Contracting Officer will consider the maturity of the program, the degree of risk to be collectively shared by the contractor and Government, and other factors. For cost reimbursement contracts (including T&M contracts) the risk is on the Government. For FFP contracts the contractor bears the risk. Selection of the appropriate contract type will provide an incentive for effective and efficient contractor performance.

 

B.4 Source Selection Procedures. Discuss the source-selection procedures for the acquisition, including the timing for submission and evaluation of proposals, and the relationship of evaluation factors to the attainment of the acquisition objectives. When an EVMS is required and a pre-award Integrated Baseline Review (IBR) is contemplated, the acquisition plan must discuss how the pre-award IBR will be considered in the source selection decision; how it will be conducted in the source selection process; and whether offerors will be directly compensated for the costs of participating in a pre-award IBR.

 

a. Source Selection Authority (SSA).

The source selection policies and procedures of FAR Subpart 15.3, including the designation of an SSA, do not apply to orders placed against GSA Federal Supply Schedule contracts, orders placed against Blanket Purchase Agreements (BPAs), or task and delivery orders placed against indefinite delivery contracts (IAW DARS 15.303(S-90)). DISA SSA delegations are indicated in DARS 15.303(a).

 

b. Source Selection Plan (SSP). The Planner shall address the evaluation factors of the SSP. Also specify whether you are using the Best Value Tradeoff Analysis source selection process or Lowest Priced Technically Acceptable source selection Process.

 

c. Indefinite-Delivery, Indefinite-Quantity (IDIQ) Contracts. Source selection procedures would be appropriate for the basic contract award, but are not required for individual task/delivery orders for multiple award contracts which will use the Fair Opportunity Process.

 

d. Past Performance. Past Performance Information (PPI) is a predictor of an offeror's ability to perform successfully under the contract. Therefore, it is required that PPI shall be evaluated in all source selections for negotiated competitive acquisitions over $1,000,000 for Information Technology, Telecommunications Equipment or Services unless the CO determines that it is inappropriate and documents the rationale. Note that waiving past performance in source selection is relatively infrequent. (Thresholds for other classes of acquisitions are located at DFARS 42.1502.).

 

The AP shall address how PPI will be evaluated in the source selection. The evaluation should take into account PPI regarding predecessor companies, key personnel who have relevant experience, and/or subcontractors that will perform major or critical aspects of the requirement. The evaluation should also include the past performance of the offerors in complying with subcontracting plan goals.

This will facilitate an easier transition to a firm-fixed-price contact because a cost history will be developed for a recurring definitive requirement.

b. Performance-Based Acquisition. PBA is required to the "maximum extent practicable" for all service acquisitions. The objective of PBA is to define requirements in terms of performance objectives and allow contractors latitude in determining how to meet those objectives. It is an approach for acquiring what is required and placing the responsibility on how it is accomplished on the contractor. PBA is intended to ensure that required performance quality levels are achieved and that total payments are directly related to the quality of services received. PBA at a minimum should contain the following elements: a performance based work statement, measurable performance standards, remedies for less than satisfactory performance, and a performance assessment plan.

 

The AP must provide rationale if a performance-based contract will not be used or if a performance based contract for services is contemplated for award on other than a firm fixed price basis.
